A shape sliced by lines parallel to its base keeps the same area when the pieces are moved parallel to this same base line. Similarly, a volume sliced by planes parallel to its base keeps the same volume when the pieces are moved parallel its base plane.

The area of a rectangle is the lenght of the base, multiplied by its height perpendicular to the base, $\b⋅\h$. By increasing the number of slices to infinity, it is obvious that for any parallelogram, the formula is also: $$A=\b\h$$

Half of a rectangle, split in a straight line from opposite vertices, is a right triangle. By Cavalieri's principle stated above, all triangles with the same base and height have the same area: Half a parallelogram. $$A={1/2}\b\h$$

The constant $π$ is defined as the ratio between the perimeter P and the diameter D.

$π=\P/\D$      $\D=2\r$      $\P=2π\r$

For a regular n-sided polygon the perimeter is the lenght of a base b, times n: $\P=\b⋅{n}$
Factor the area of one of its triangles to get $A={1/2}\b\h⋅{n}$, equivalent to $A={1/2}\h⋅\P$.
Letting n reach infinity to get a circle, sides s and h tend to a radius r, so $A={1/2}\r⋅\P$.
Using the formula for the perimeter of a circle, we can write the area as

$A={1/2}\r⋅2π\r$      which simplifies as      $A=π\r^2$.

The volume of an n-sided polygonal prism is: $V=\text"Base Area"⋅\h$,
its surface area is: $A=(\text"Base Perimeter"⋅\h) + (2⋅\text"Base Area")$.

A circular prism, (a cylinder) has volume $V=π\r^2⋅\h$ and a surface area of $A=(2π\r⋅\h) + (2⋅π\r^2)$.

Note that if a cylinder has the same height as its width, $(\text"Height"=\text"Diameter"=2\r)$, then the volume could be written as: $V=π\r^2⋅2\r= 2π\r^3$, its surface area: $A=(4π\r^2) + (2⋅π\r^2) = 6π\r^2$.

An open pyramid, with a regular n-sided polygon base has an area $A={1/2}\b{s}⋅n$
The perimeter of the base can be written as $\P=\b{n}$ so the area is also $A={1/2}\P{s}$
Letting n reach infinity to get a cone, the perimeter of the base is then $\P=2π\r$
So the surface area of an open cone is then $A={1/2}⋅2π\r⋅\s$, or $A=π\r\s$
If it becomes flat like a disk, s then equals r, so its area is $A=π\r⋅\r$, which is $A = π\r^2$

We can approximate a pyramid with $n$ regular polygonal prisms.
With rectangular prisms, each slice x has volume $V=x\L/{n}⋅x\W/{n}⋅{\H/n}$
The sum is then $V=1^2{LW}/n^2{H/n}+2^2{LW}/n^2{H/n}+...+n^2{LW}/n^2{H/n}$
Factor out ${LWH}/n^3$ to get $V={LWH}/n^3⋅(1^2+2^2+...+n^2)$
Recognize the sum of power series $∑↙{i=1}↖n i^2 = {n(2n+1)(n+1)}/6$
So that $V={LWH}/n^3⋅{n(2n+1)(n+1)}/6$, cancel an $n$ to get
$V={LWH}/n^2⋅{(2n+1)(n+1)}/6$, multiply out the binomes,
$V={LWH}/n^2⋅{2n^2+3n+1}/6$, separate as $V={LWH}/n^2⋅({n^2}/3+n/2+1/6)$, and cancel $1/{n^2}$ to get
$V={LWH}⋅(1/3+1/{2n}+1/{6n^2})$. By increasing the number of slices, n, reach infinity, $V={LWH}⋅(1/3+1/{2∞}+1/{6∞^2})$ which is $V={LWH}⋅1/3$ where $LW$ is the area of the base.


$V={LWH}⋅1/3$ being the formula for the volume of a rectangular pyramid, where $LW$ is the area of the base, we can deduce that $V={(\text"Base Area")⋅H}⋅1/3$ is the formula for the volume of any pyramid.

To get a hold of the properties of spheres, it is necessary to prove that the shapes of horizontal sections of an inverted cone have the same area, at all heights, of that of horizontal sections of the outside of a hemisphere inscribed in a cylinder:

First, we acknowledge that the area of a horizontal section of an inverted cone, at a given height h, where the height of the cone is equal to its radius, is computed as $A=π\h^2$.

Next, we subtract from the area of a section of a cylinder, $π\r^2$, the area of a section of a hemisphere at height h, $π(√{\r^2-\h^2})^2$ to get the area of the ring outside the hemisphere:
$$A=π\r^2 - π(√{\r^2-\h^2})^2$$

It can be simplified to $A=π\r^2 - π(\r^2-\h^2)$, and further to $A=π\r^2 - π\r^2 + π\h^2$, which is $A=π\h^2$.

Having shown that those areas are equal at all heights, $π\h^2$, it follows that the volume of a cone and that of the outside of a hemisphere inscribed in a cylinder are also equal.

Then from the volume of a cylinder, $\h⋅π\r^2$, if we subtract the volume of a cone of same height and radius, $1/3⋅\h⋅π\r^2$, we obtain the formula for the volume of a hemisphere: $2/3⋅h⋅π\r^2$, or if the height equals the radius, $2/3⋅π\r^3$. Doubling this value gives the formula for a whole sphere: $V=4/3π\r^3$.

If we consider a sphere to be made up of infinitely thin pyramids of height r having their apex at the center and their bases facing outward as the outside surface. We can write a formula for the volume $V=4/3π\r^3$ as: ${1/3b_1\r}+{1/3b_2\r}+{1/3b_3\r}+...$
By factoring out common parts of the terms we get: ${1/3\r}⋅(b_1+b_2+b_3 + ... )$

The sum of the bases, $(b_1+b_2+b_3+...)$ is the outside area of the sphere, which can be isolated by dividing the volume's formula $V=4/3πr^3$, by ${1/3\r}$, so that, the surface area of a sphere can be found: $$A=4π\r^2$$

The theorem of pythagore says that for a right triangle, the length of the hypotenuse squared is the sum of the squares of the other two sides.

It directly follows from the definition of sine and cosine, that $cos^2\(θ)+sin^2(θ)=1$.

Multiples of $π/2$ angles (90 degrees) yield trigonometric values of either 1 or 0.

An angle of pi/4, (45 degrees), has equal sine and cosine values for being exactly in the middle range of both axis. To find this value 'x', we start with the hypotenuse squared which is equal to twice that value squared: $1^2=x^2+x^2$.

This is equivalent to $1=√{x^2+x^2}$ or $1=√{2x^2}$.
Squaring both sides gives: $1=2x^2$. Dividing both sides by two: $1/2=x^2$.
Isolatings x by taking the square root gives: $1/{√{2}}=x$. Rationalizing the denominator yields ${√{2}/2$.

Multiples of $π/6$ angles have sine and cosine values that can be deduced from the pythagorean theorem by starting with an equilateral triange of side length 1.

We cut that triangle in half with a vertical line to keep only one of the right triangles. Its base is then exactly half the hypotenuse: $1/2$. Its remaining side x is then $x=√{1^2-{1/2}^2}$, which is also $x=√{1-{1/4}}$ the equivalent of $x=√{3/4}$ or $√{3}/2$

The simple version of arithmetic series written as $ ∑↙{i=1}↖n i$ is a sum of numbers from 1 to n, $s$ such that
$ s= 1+2+3+...+{n-2}+{n-1}+n $ that can also be writen in reverse order :
$ s= n+ {n-1} +{n-2} + ... +3 +2+1 $ so that we can 'pair up' n terms from both versions as n+1.

$2s=(1+n)+(1+n)+(1+n)+...+(1+n)+(1+n)$ Repeated n times is $2s=n(n+1)$
Dividing both sides by 2 gives $$∑↙{i=1}↖n i={n(n+1)}/2$$

A more general arithmetic series such as $∑↙{i=a}↖b \c i+\d$ can be decomposed as : $ \c⋅∑↙{i=a}↖b i + ∑↙{i=a}↖b \d $

The critical part of the arithmetic series is then $∑↙{i=a}↖b i= S$.

If we define $S$ as $a+(a+1)+(a+2)+...+(b-1)+b$
and also show $S$ as $b+(b-1)+...+(a+2)+(a+1)+a$ in reverse order.

Then $2S$ is $(a+b)$ repeated a number of times equal to $(b-a+1)$, dividing by 2 yields $S$ :

$$∑↙{i=a}↖b i= {(a+b)(b-a+1)}/2$$

Example: The series $[-3,-1,1,3,5]$ in sigma notation is either $∑↙{i=-1}↖3 2i-1 $ or $∑↙{i=-2}↖2 2i+1$ .

The first version can be simplified as $2⋅∑↙{i=-1}↖3 i + ∑↙{i=-1}↖3 -1 \minsize 2{⇒} 10 - 5 \minsize 2{⇒} 5 $

The second version can be simplified as $2⋅∑↙{i=-2}↖2 i + ∑↙{i=-2}↖2 1 \minsize 2{⇒} 0 + 5 \minsize 2{⇒} 5 $

The formula given above as $∑↙{i=a}↖b i= {(a+b)(b-a+1)}/2$ can be used in both cases to get the same result, 5.

Infinite Geometric Series $∑↙{i=0}↖∞ ar^i $

Factor out 'a' and expand $∑↙{i=0}↖∞ r^i = S$, consider that :

          $ S = 1+r+r^2+r^3 + ... $       If we multiply the sum again S by r we get :
         $ rS = r+r^2+r^3+r^4 + ... $      Subtracting rS from the previous sum S, only 1 remains :
   $ S-rS = 1 $
$ S(1-r) = 1 $
          $ S = 1/{1-r} $ Bringing back 'a' gives $ ∑↙{i=0}↖∞ ar^i = a/{1-r} $ which converges when $|r|<1$

Finite Geometric Series $∑↙{i=0}↖n ar^i $ with the same considerations.

Factor out 'a' and expand $∑↙{i=0}↖n r^i = S$, consider that :

$ S(1-r) = 1-r^{n+1} $
          $ S = {1- r^{n+1}}/{1-r} $ Bringing back 'a' gives $ ∑↙{i=0}↖n ar^i = a{1- r^{n+1}}/{1-r}$

  • Newton's Method

Newton's method is an iterative process that approximates the zeros of a continuous function.

A function can be setup, so that finding it's zeros yields a useful value.
For example, the value of $√2$ can be approximated by finding the zeros of $y=x^2-2$.



A graphical explanation of Newton's method can be described on a cartesian plane, starting with a reasonable first guess: $x_0$.

Considering the value of the function at $x_0$, the tangent line to the function at that point gives the next guess, $x_1$, where it crosses the x-axis.



Another iteration is computed by taking again the value of the function at the new $x_1$ and looking at its tangent line, for its own x-intercept, $x_2$

The general formula for Newton's method to find the next $x$ starting with $x_0$, starting with the equation of a line in point-slope form : $$y-y_0 = m(x-x_0)$$ We know the tangent touches $f(x)$ at $(x_0,f(x_0))$, that the slope is obviously $f'(x)$,
and the line will cross the x-axis when $y=0$. So we now rewrite as $$0-f(x_0)=f'(x_0)(x-x_0)$$ distributing on the right side, $$0-f(x_0)=f'(x_0)x- f'(x_0)x_0$$ moving terms around to isolate the one with $x$, $$f'(x_0)x=f'(x_0)x_0-f(x_0)$$ and dividing both sides by $f'(x_0)$ yields $$x=x_0 - {f(x_0)}/{f'(x_0)}$$

Starting with Taylor's formula for $f(x)$ near zero: $f(0){x^0}/0!+f'(0){x^1}/1!+f''(0){x^2}/2!+f'''(0){x^3}/3!+...$
also written as $f(0)+f'(0)x+f''(0){x^2}/2!+f'''(0){x^3}/3!+...$ we consider those of sin(x), cos(x) and $\e^x$

The cycle of derivatives for sin(x) are : cos(x), -sin(x), -cos(x), and back to sin(x).
Starting with sin(x), we evaluate those at zero to get : 0, 1, 0, -1, ...
It is now easy to write the Taylor series approximation for sin(x) near zero : $ x-{x^3}/3!+{x^5}/5!-{x^7}/7!+... $

The cycle of derivatives for cos(x) are : -sin(x), -cos(x), sin(x)and back to cos(x).
Starting with cos(x), we evaluate those at zero to get : 1, 0, -1, 0 ...
The Taylor series approximation for cos(x) near zero is then : $ 1-{x^2}/2!+{x^4}/4!-{x^6}/6!+... $

It is well known that the derivatives of $\e^x$ are all $\e^x$ and evaluating $\e^x$ where x is zero gives 1.
It is then easy to see, using Taylor's formula, that $\e^x ≈ 1+x+{x^2}/2!+{x^3}/3!+{x^4}/4!+...$

The Taylor series approximation of $\e^x$ seems to be the sum of those of sin(x) and cos(x) but the signs do not match exactly, they alternate two by two.

A pattern that matches this sign rotation is the consecutive powers of i :
$i^0 = 1$, $i^1 = i$, $i^2 = -1$, $i^3 = -i$, $i^4 = 1$ and back to $i^5 = i$ ...

A property of expanded series is composition, so we can replace the 'x' by 'ix' in $\e^x$ like so : $\e^{ix}$
yielding $\e^{ix} ≈ 1+{ix}+{(ix)^2}/2!+{(ix)^3}/3!+{(ix)^4}/4!+{(ix)^5}/5!+...$
which is $\e^{ix} ≈ 1+{ix}-{x^2}/2!-{ix^3}/3!+{x^4}/4!+{ix^5}/5!-...$
The odd ones, matching those from the series for sin(x) have an imaginary factor so we can write :
$\e^{ix} = \cos(x) + i\sin(x)$. If we make x=$π$, we get $\e^{iπ} = -1 + 0$, or, Euler's Identity : $\e^{iπ} = -1$

Taking the square root on both sides of Euler's Identity: $√{e^{iπ}}=√{-1}$ reduces to $e^{iπ/2}=i$.

Taking the log on both sides $\ln({e^{iπ/2}})=\ln(i)$, simplifies to $iπ/2=\ln(i)$.

Multiplying both sides by 2, $iπ=2\ln(i)$ gives $iπ=\ln(i^2)$ hence $iπ=\ln(-1)$.

Raising $e^{iπ/2}=i$ to the power of i gives $({e^{iπ/2}})^i=i^i$ or $e^{i^2π/2}=i^i$, thus $e^{-π/2}=i^i$, a real number.
